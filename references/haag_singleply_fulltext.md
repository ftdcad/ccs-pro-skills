are similar for thermoplastic and thermoset membranes. Membranes can be  

mechanically fastened, adhered,
 


or ballasted 

over the roof substrate. With traditional mechanical attachment, the fasteners are in the seams.

as coastal areas or tall buildings, may preclude use of some securement schemes. The local building code
 


would set forth specific requirements or restrictions.

Section 3: Hail & Wind Damage
 


Substrate Effects
 


Hail can sometimes dent, fracture, or puncture the substrate layers without fracturing the roof membrane. This is
 


most common below flexible single-ply membranes in good condition over relatively compressible substrates (such
 


as foam insulation boards) or relatively thin and rigid layers (such as gypsum or cement boards). Concerns with this
 


type of condition can involve whether there has been any loss of insulation effectiveness (R-value), and for adhered
 


membranes, whether there has been any loss of adhesive bond. Generally, hail has to be quite large to cause
 


significant damage to substrate layers.
 


With regard to R-value, note that the effective R-value for the roofing system is based on all layers from the roof
 


deck to the membrane, and not just a single component. Due to the numerous possible combinations of single-
 


ply membranes and substrates, not all possible situations can be covered in this guide. In some circumstances,
 


substrate layers can be directly examined through test cut openings and other testing procedures can be
 


considered. In our experience, unless a  

thermal bridge 

is created by fracture through the entire thickness of
 


insulation or cover board layer, the reduction in R-value is insignificant. If testing for loss of R-value due to hail
 


indentations in insulation, attempt to compare specimen(s) with notable indentations to those without.
 


With regard to loss of adhesion, consideration must be given to the approximate percentage, if any, of surface area
 


with reduced adhesive bond due to hailstone impacts. It should be noted that existing adhered roofing membrane
 


systems do not have 100% adhesive bond due to the irregularities in the surfaces, joints between substrate boards,
 


depressions in the substrate at fastener plates, installation variations, etc. In most cases, any change in adhesive
 


bond related to hail impact is minimal compared to the existing variations across the roof.

Section 3: Hail & Wind Damage
 


Introduction - Wind
 


Wind damage normally occurs first at areas of the roof subjected to the greatest uplift pressures due to aerodynamic
 


effects: windward corners and edges. Greater wind speeds, and hence, greater wind-generated pressures, are found
 


higher from the ground. The exposure of a building to wind also plays a significant role in the wind pressures affecting
 


the roof. Variables in exposure include the surrounding topography, the presence of vegetation, and the size and
 


proximity of other buildings. In addition, the presence of openings to the building interior will affect the wind pressure
 


on the roof.
 


The progression of wind-caused damage typically begins with scouring of gravel ballast from windward edges and

Section 3: Hail & Wind Damage
 


Introduction - Wind
 


Wind damage normally occurs first at areas of the roof subjected to the greatest uplift pressures due to aerodynamic
 


effects: windward corners and edges. Greater wind speeds, and hence, greater wind-generated pressures, are found
 


higher from the ground. The exposure of a building to wind also plays a significant role in the wind pressures affecting
 


the roof. Variables in exposure include the surrounding topography, the presence of vegetation, and the size and
 


proximity of other buildings. In addition, the presence of openings to the building interior will affect the wind pressure
 


on the roof.
 


The progression of wind-caused damage typically begins with scouring of gravel ballast from windward edges and
 


corners, distorting or tearing away metal edge flashings, and progresses to ballooning, peeling, or tearing the
 


membrane from the roof. In addition, single-ply membrane roofing can be damaged by wind-blown debris, which can
 


cause scuffs, tears, or punctures.

EPDM:

where the roof covering is interrupted or terminated.
 


Fleece (Backing): 

 

Mats of felt composed of fibers (usually non-woven polyester fibers) used as a membrane backer or separator sheet.
 


Insulation: 

 

Any variety of materials designed to reduce the flow of heat, either from or into a building.

Thermal Bridge:  

 

An area of an object (frequently in a building) which has a significantly higher heat transfer than the surrounding materials resulting in an overall
 


reduction in thermal insulation of the object or building.
 


Thermoplastic:  

 

Polymers which soften when heated and harden when cooled. This process is repetitive provided the material is not heated above the point at which
 


decomposition occurs. Thermoplastic single-ply membranes are most commonly TPO or PVC.
 


Thermoplastic
 


Polyolefin (TPO): 

 

A thermoplastic membrane made from a copolymer or blend of polyethylene and polypropylene polymers.
 


Thermoset:  

 

A material that solidifies or “sets” irreversibly when heated. This property is usually associated with crosslinking of the molecules induced by heat or
 


radiation. EPDM membrane is a thermoset material.
 


Threshold (Hail Size):  

 

The smallest hail size where physical damage would be reasonably expected to occur. Occurrence of threshold-size hail does not guarantee that the
 


roofing material will be damaged.
 


Vegetative Roof Assembly:  

 

A substrate (roof deck) and a vegetative roof system.
 


Vegetative Roof
 


System (VRS): 

 

A roof area of plantings/landscaping installed above a waterproofed substrate at any building level that is over habitable space. A VRS consists of a
 


waterproofing system and its associated components (protection course, root barrier, drainage layer, thermal insulation and aeration layer) and an
 


overburden of growth medium and plantings.
 


Water-shedding:  

 

The ability of individual, overlapping components to resist the passage of water without hydrostatic pressure.
