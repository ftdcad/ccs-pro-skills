# Florida -- State PRO Reference
**Source:** Merlin Law Group State Guide (Florida Edition, for Licensed Public Adjusters)
**Last verified:** 2026-03-04

## 1. Notice of Loss

**Deadline:** Immediate.

- Failure to give timely notice of loss is a legal basis for denial of recovery under the policy. *Ideal Mut. Ins. Co. v. Waldrep*, 400 So.2d 782 (Fla. 3d DCA 1981).
- A claim or reopened property claim is **barred unless notice is given to the insurer within 1 year after the date of loss**. A supplemental property claim is barred unless notice is given within **18 months** after the date of loss. F.S. 627.70132(2).
- For hurricanes, tornadoes, windstorms, severe rain, or other weather-related events, the "date of loss" is the date the hurricane made landfall or the event is verified by NOAA. F.S. 627.70132(3).
- Sinkhole claims are barred unless notice is given within **2 years** after the policyholder knew or reasonably should have known about the loss. F.S. 627.706(5).

**Late notice defense:** Where an insured breaches the notice provisions, prejudice to the insurer will be **presumed but may be rebutted** by showing the insurer was not prejudiced. An explicit deadline does not abrogate the notice-prejudice rule. *State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. v. Curran*, 135 So. 3d 1071 (Fla. 2014); *Kramer v. State Farm Florida Ins. Co.*, 95 So. 3d 303 (Fla. 4th DCA 2012).

## 2. Proof of Loss

**Deadline:** Look to policy provision -- typically 30 days, 60 days, or upon request.

- Failure to file a statement of proof of loss within 60 days was held to be a breach of contract and grounds for denial. *Starling v. Allstate*, 956 So.2d 511 (Fla. 5th DCA 2007).
- **Carrier form-furnishing obligation:** Not explicitly addressed for non-flood claims in this guide. (Florida flood follows federal NFIP rules -- 60-day proof of loss, standard FEMA form required.)
- The insurer must send the policyholder a copy of any detailed estimate of the loss made by the insurer's adjuster within **7 days** after the estimate is generated. F.S. 627.70131(3)(e).
- Public adjuster must provide the insured a written, itemized estimate of repairs within **60 days** after the date of the PA contract. F.S. 626.845(12)(a).
- Contract and Proof of Loss must contain specific language that submitting a false or misleading Proof of Loss or estimate is a 3rd degree felony. F.S. 626.8796 & F.S. 626.8797.

## 3. Pre-Suit Notice Requirement + Cure Window

**Notice of Intent to File Lawsuit:** F.S. 627.70152(3).

- Applies to residential, commercial, and surplus lines insurers.
- Does **not** apply to lawsuits brought by assignees.
- Providing the Department of Financial Services (DFS) with written notice on their form is a **condition precedent** to filing suit.
- Cannot be filed by a public adjuster (constitutes practice of law) -- **must be filed by an attorney**.
- Notice must be given **10 business days before suit** and served within the statute of limitations.

**Civil Remedy Notice (CRN) for bad faith:** F.S. 624.155.

- Filing a CRN is a **condition precedent** to filing suit for bad faith.
- Insurer has **60 days from receipt** of the CRN to pay the claim or correct the violation.
- Time does not start until a complete CRN is filed.
- CRN must be on a form provided by the Department and must include: statute number, specific statutory language violated, facts and circumstances, names of individuals involved, specific policy language relevant to the violation, and a statement that the notice is given to perfect the right to pursue the civil remedy under 624.155.
- CRN **may not be filed within 60 days after appraisal is invoked** by any party in a residential property insurance claim.
- Statute of limitations is **tolled 65 days** by mailing the CRN to the insurance company and the Department.

## 4. Carrier Acknowledgment Deadline

**Not explicitly stated as a standalone deadline in this guide.**

The guide focuses on the 90-day pay/deny deadline (see Field 5). Florida's general claims handling practices under F.S. 626.9541 and the Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act require prompt acknowledgment, but the Merlin Guide does not cite a specific day count for acknowledgment alone.

**Web search recommended** for the specific acknowledgment timeline under Florida insurance regulations.

## 5. Carrier Investigation / Payment Deadline

**90 days** from notice of a property insurance claim (residential property or commercial property 10,000 sq ft or less).

- Insurer must **pay or deny a claim within 90 days** of notice. F.S. 627.70121(5)(a).
- Any payment after 90 days bears **interest from the date the insurer received notice** of the claim. F.S. 627.70121(5)(a).

## 6. SOL -- Breach of Contract

**5 years from date of loss** for breach of property insurance contract. F.S. 95.11(2)(e).

- 4 years for agent negligence. F.S. 95.11(3).
- FIGA claims: Suit must be filed within **1 year** after the deadline for filing claims with the receiver of the insolvent insurer. F.S. 631.68.
- New/reopened claims must have notice given within 1 year of loss; supplemental claims within 18 months. F.S. 627.70132(2).

## 7. SOL -- Bad Faith

**Not explicitly stated as a standalone limitation period in this guide.**

- Bad faith claim not ripe until damages are determined. *Liberty Mutual Ins. Co. v. Farm, Inc.*, 754 So.2d 865 (Fla. 3rd DCA 2000).
- CRN filing tolls the statute of limitations by 65 days.
- Florida bad faith claims are generally governed by F.S. 95.11(3)(f) -- **4 years** for statutory liability -- but this specific citation is not in the Merlin Guide. **Web search recommended to confirm current SOL for bad faith.**

## 8. Appraisal

- **Enforceable.** *Southern Home Ins. Co. v. Faulkner*, 49 So. 542 (Fla. 1909).
- **Binding.**
- **No general time frame** for invoking appraisal. Once invoked, look to the policy for the time requirement on appraiser appointment.
- **Can be waived.** *Bankers v. Brady*, 765 So.2d 870 (Fla. 5th DCA 2000) -- insurance company could not demand appraisal after PA and company adjuster agreed on loss amount.
- **Condition precedent to filing suit** if appraisal clause in policy is invoked. *New Amsterdam Cas. Co. v. Blackshear*, 116 So. 289 (Fla. 1934).
- Insureds cannot demand appraisal until they have complied with all post-loss policy provisions, including notice of loss, EUO, and providing all reasonable information requested by the insurer.
- Causation may be analyzed by appraisers, but **coverage disputes are reserved for courts**.
- CRN may not be filed within 60 days after appraisal is invoked in a residential property insurance claim.
- Critical to read the appraisal clause in the specific policy for notice requirements, time frames, number of appraisers, and appraiser appointment requirements.

## 9. EUO (Examination Under Oath)

- Read the insurance policy -- many policies are now expanding the definition of who the policyholder must provide for EUO.
- **Books & Records:** Insurer has very broad discretion in what can be demanded.
- **Practical guidance:** Provide everything the insurance company asks for regarding books and records, or run the risk of having the claim denied.
- The guide does not explicitly state EUO cooperation as a statutory condition precedent, but it is universally treated as a policy condition precedent in Florida. Failure to comply risks denial.

## 10. Matching Regulation

**Yes -- Florida has a matching statute.** F.S. 626.9744.

Unless otherwise provided by the policy, when a homeowner's insurance policy provides for adjustment and settlement of first-party losses based on repair or replacement cost, and the replaced items **do not match in quality, color, or size**, the insurer shall make reasonable repairs or replacement of items in **adjoining areas**.

In determining the extent of repairs/replacement in adjoining areas, the insurer may consider:
- The cost of repairing or replacing the undamaged portions of the property
- The degree of uniformity that can be achieved without such cost
- The remaining useful life of the undamaged portion
- Other relevant factors

**Citation:** F.S. 626.9744.

## Notes

### Valued Policy Law
F.S. 627.702 applies in Florida:
- For **covered perils resulting in total loss**, payment is the amount for which the property was insured and premium charged/paid.
- For **partial loss by lightning or fire**, insurer is liable for the actual amount of loss, not to exceed the policy amount.
- Does NOT apply where: multiple insurers cover the same building (undisclosed), blanket policies cover two or more properties, builder's risk policies, coinsurance clauses apply, or personal property (except mobile/manufactured homes).
- If loss is caused by a covered AND non-covered peril, the valued policy law does not apply -- insurer is liable only for damage from the covered peril.

### Attorney Fees
The guide does not detail attorney fee provisions, but notes that in CRN/bad faith context, the insurer's 60-day cure window and the CRN process are the gateways to statutory remedies which historically included fee-shifting (significantly modified by SB 2A in 2022 and subsequent reforms).

### FIGA Limitations
- FIGA may not pay attorney or public adjuster fees for sinkhole losses. F.S. 631.54(3)(c).

### Interest on Late Payment
Any payment after the 90-day deadline bears interest from the date the insurer received notice of the claim. F.S. 627.70121(5)(a).

### Claim Definitions (Post-2022 Reform)
F.S. 627.70132(1)(a)-(b) introduced new definitions for "reopened" and "supplemental" claims with separate notice deadlines (1 year vs. 18 months).
