# Matching Regulations by State — Reference Chart

**Source:** Matthiesen, Wickert & Lehrer, S.C. — "Matching Regulations" and Laws Affecting Homeowners' Property Claims in All 50 States
**Original document date:** Last Updated 1/13/22
**Extracted for CCS Pro Chain:** 2026-03-04
**Purpose:** State PRO reference for matching arguments in Loss Below PRO and related pipeline steps

---

## How to Read This Chart

- **Has Matching Reg?** = Whether the state has a statute, regulation, insurance bulletin, or binding case law that addresses matching/uniformity in RCV claims
- **Citation** = The specific statute, regulation, admin code, bulletin, or case law reference
- **Key Rule** = Plain-English summary of what the rule requires or what the court held
- **Notes** = Special conditions, limitations, line-of-sight rules, private right of action issues, or tactical notes for CCS adjusters

**Important context:** The NAIC Model Regulation (MDL-902, 1997) Section 9(A)(2) states that when replacement items do not match in quality, color, or size, the insurer shall replace items in the area to conform to a "reasonably uniform appearance" for both interior and exterior losses. Many states adopted some version of this. However, most states do NOT give homeowners a private right of action under these regulations — meaning you cannot sue under the regulation itself, but the regulation can be used as evidence of unreasonable conduct.

## States With Strong Matching Protections

These states have statutes, regulations, or strong case law that clearly support full or area-wide replacement when matching materials are unavailable. CCS adjusters should know these for Loss Below PRO arguments.

1. **Alaska (AK)** — Regulation requires uniform appearance replacement. Insured only pays deductible.
2. **California (CA)** — Regulation requires replacement in damaged area for reasonably uniform appearance. (No private right of action, but strong regulatory backing.)
3. **Connecticut (CT)** — Statute (not just regulation) requires matching of adjacent items. Statutory force is stronger than regulations alone. (Limited to "adjacent" items per case law.)
4. **Florida (FL)** — Statute requires reasonable repairs/replacement in adjoining area. KEY STATE FOR CCS.
5. **Iowa (IA)** — Regulation with line-of-sight matching requirement.
6. **Louisiana (LA)** — Statute requires restoration to original condition. Court required full carpet replacement under RCV.
7. **Montana (MT)** — Two Commissioner memorandums requiring matching. If materials unavailable, existing must be replaced to achieve match.
8. **Nebraska (NE)** — Regulation for uniform appearance. (Courts interpret narrowly — watch for limited scope.)
9. **Ohio (OH)** — Regulation for reasonably comparable appearance. (Burden on insured to prove mismatch.)
10. **Rhode Island (RI)** — Regulation explicitly intends full loss coverage. Strong language against carriers using ordinance/law restrictions.
11. **Tennessee (TN)** — Regulation PLUS supportive case law. Court approved replacing undamaged shingles for aesthetics.
12. **Utah (UT)** — Regulation for uniform appearance. Insured only pays deductible.
13. **Vermont (VT)** — Regulation with line-of-sight matching requirement.

### States With Favorable Case Law (No Statute/Regulation)

These states lack formal matching regulations but have case law that supports matching arguments:

- **Colorado (CO)** — Court required insurer to pay for skim coating to create uniform appearance.
- **District of Columbia (DC)** — Ambiguous policy read in favor of insured; "comparable material" means property that looks the same.
- **Indiana (IN)** — Court required shingle matching under RCV policy based on expert testimony about diminished value.
- **Minnesota (MN)** — "Color mismatch" = direct physical loss. Building is covered as a whole. Very favorable case law.
- **Missouri (MO)** — Ambiguous "like kind and quality" language interpreted to require matching. RCV = higher premium = higher protection.

Adjusters should be aware that these states have case law that actively works against matching arguments:

## Key Tactical Notes for CCS Adjusters

1. **RCV vs. ACV matters everywhere.** Many matching regulations only apply to Replacement Cost Value policies. If the insured has an ACV policy, matching arguments are significantly weaker.

2. **"Reasonably uniform" is NOT "perfect match."** Courts consistently hold that a perfect match is not required — the standard is "reasonably uniform appearance" or "reasonably comparable appearance."

3. **Private right of action is rare.** Most states do not give homeowners a private right of action under matching regulations. The regulation can be used as *evidence* of unreasonable conduct but cannot be the basis for a standalone lawsuit.

4. **Burden of proof on insured.** In states like Ohio, the insured must provide evidence beyond mere opinion that replacement materials would not result in a reasonably comparable appearance.

5. **Policy language is paramount.** In states without matching regulations, the specific policy terms, conditions, and definitions control. Watch for "Common Construction" provisions, cosmetic damage exclusions, and "Roof Surfacing Loss Percentage Tables."

6. **Discontinued materials strengthen matching arguments.** When the original materials have been discontinued by the manufacturer, matching arguments are strongest because it becomes impossible to find replacement material of "like kind and quality."

7. **Florida is the priority state.** With F.S.A. sec. 626.9744 requiring reasonable matching in adjoining areas, Florida gives CCS the strongest statutory tool for matching arguments in its primary market.
