# Colorado — State PRO Reference
**Source:** Merlin Law Group State Guide
**Last verified:** 2026-03-04

## 1. Notice of Loss
**Deadline:** Immediate (which means within a reasonable time).

- "Immediate notice" means within a **reasonable time**. *Barclay v. London Guarantee & Acc. Co.*, 105 P. 865 (Colo. 1909).
- Notice of a claim may be expressly made a condition precedent to recovery on a policy. *Security Mut. Cas. Co. v. Century Cas. Co.*, 531 F.2d 974 (10th Cir. 1976).
- **Prejudice rule (two-step test):** Delay in giving notice is not fatal to recovery unless the insurer is prejudiced by the delay. Colorado uses a **two-step approach**: (1) Was the notice untimely and the delay unreasonable? (2) If yes, was the insurer prejudiced by the untimely notice? *Gregory v. Safeco Ins. Co. of Am.*, 2024 WL 1040531 (Colo. 2024).

## 2. Proof of Loss
**Deadline:** Per policy provision (commonly 30 days, 60 days, or upon request).

- Proof of loss is a condition precedent to recovery on a policy.
- **Prejudice rule:** Delay in giving proof of loss is not fatal to recovery unless the insurer is **materially and substantially disadvantaged**. Non-cooperation constitutes breach of an insurance policy only if material and substantial disadvantage to the insurer is proved. *Ahmadi v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 22 P.3d 576 (Colo. App. 2001).

## 5. Carrier Investigation/Payment Deadline
**Unreasonable delay/denial subjects insurer to 2x covered benefit + attorney fees.**

- **Unreasonable delay/denial statute:** In a first-party claim, if the insurer unreasonably delays or denies a claim for payment of benefits, the insured may bring an action in district court to recover **reasonable attorney's fees, court costs, and two times the covered benefit**. Colo. Rev. Stat. section 10-3-1115 through 1116.
- Claims brought under this statute have a **two-year** statute of limitations. *Alarcon v. American Family Ins. Group*, 2010 WL 2541131 (D. Colo. Jun. 18, 2010).
- (Note: The guide does not specify a day-count investigation/payment deadline. Colorado DOI regulations and Colo. Rev. Stat. section 10-3-1104 likely set specific timeframes — web search required.)

## 6. SOL — Breach of Contract
**3 years from date breach is discovered or should have been discovered. Cannot be shortened by contract for homeowner policies issued on or after January 1, 2014.**

- **Breach of contract SOL: 3 years** from the date the breach is discovered or should have been discovered by the exercise of reasonable diligence. Colo. Rev. Stat. section 13-80-101.
- **Anti-shortening provision:** Any provision in a homeowner's insurance policy issued on or after January 1, 2014 that requires the insured to bring suit within a shorter time period than the applicable statute of limitations is **unenforceable**. Colo. Rev. Stat. section 10-4-110.8(12). This is a major policyholder protection — carriers cannot shorten the 3-year window in homeowner policies.

## 7. SOL — Bad Faith
**2 years from the date both the injury and its cause are known or should have been known.**

- Bad faith SOL: **2 years** from the date both the injury and its cause are known or should have been known through the exercise of reasonable diligence. Colo. Rev. Stat. section 13-80-102(1).

### Agent Negligence SOL
**2 years.** Colo. Rev. Stat. section 13-80-102.

## 8. Appraisal
**Enforceable. Binding. Usually no time frame for invocation. Causation may be analyzed by appraisers.**

- Appraisal clauses are enforceable and **binding**.
- **Waiver:** Appraisal can be waived. An insurer's denial of a fire loss constitutes waiver of the appraisal clause. *Ins. Co. of North America v. Baker*, 84 Colo. 53 (Colo. 1928).
- **Time to invoke:** Usually no time frame for invoking appraisal. Once invoked, look to policy for time requirement on appraiser appointment.
- **Causation in appraisal:** Causation may be analyzed by appraisers. *Bonbeck Parker v. Travelers Ind. Co.*, 14 F.4th 1169 (10th Cir. 2021). This is significant — unlike many states, Colorado allows appraisers to consider causation questions, not just amount of loss.
- Critical to read the specific appraisal clause in the policy for notice requirements, time frames, number of appraisers, and appointment requirements.

## 9. EUO (Examination Under Oath)
**EUO is a condition precedent to suit. Failure to comply bars recovery AND the right to bring suit.**

- **EUO compliance is a condition precedent** — failure to comply bars recovery as well as the right to bring suit under the policy. *Emenyonu v. State Farm Fire & Cas. Co.*, 885 P.2d 320 (Colo. App. 1994). This is a hard rule.
- **Cooperation clause interpretation:** There must be some disadvantage to the insurer proved, and that disadvantage must be **material and substantial**. *Brooks v. Haggard*, 481 P.2d 131 (Colo. App. 1970).
- Many policies are expanding the definition of who the policyholder must provide for EUO — read the specific policy.
- Books and records: Very broad discretion in what the insurer may demand.
- **Practical rule:** Provide everything the insurance company asks for regarding books and records, or risk having the claim denied.

## Notes

### Unreasonable Delay/Denial — 2x Damages + Attorney Fees
Colorado's unreasonable delay/denial statute (Colo. Rev. Stat. sections 10-3-1115 through 1116) is a powerful policyholder tool. If the insurer unreasonably delays or denies a first-party claim, the insured can recover:
- Reasonable attorney's fees
- Court costs
- **Two times the covered benefit**

The 2x multiplier on the covered benefit is a significant penalty structure. Claims under this statute have a separate 2-year SOL. *Alarcon v. American Family Ins. Group*, 2010 WL 2541131 (D. Colo. Jun. 18, 2010).

### Anti-Shortening Provision for Homeowner Policies
Colo. Rev. Stat. section 10-4-110.8(12) makes any contractual suit-limitation clause in homeowner policies issued on or after January 1, 2014 **unenforceable** if it shortens the statutory period. The full 3-year SOL applies. This is a major departure from states like Arizona where carriers can shorten to 1 year.

### Causation in Appraisal — 10th Circuit Ruling
*Bonbeck Parker v. Travelers Ind. Co.*, 14 F.4th 1169 (10th Cir. 2021) holds that causation may be analyzed by appraisers. This is strategically important — in many states, carriers argue that causation is a "coverage question" outside the scope of appraisal. Colorado (at least per the 10th Circuit) allows appraisers to address it.

### EUO — Hard Condition Precedent
Unlike some states where the prejudice rule softens the EUO requirement, Colorado treats EUO compliance as a hard condition precedent. *Emenyonu*, supra. Failure to comply bars both recovery and the right to sue. The cooperation clause still requires the insurer to show material and substantial disadvantage (*Brooks v. Haggard*), but the EUO itself is strictly enforced.

### Prejudice Rule — "Material and Substantial Disadvantage" Standard
Colorado's prejudice standard for notice and proof of loss requires the insurer to prove **material and substantial** disadvantage. *Ahmadi v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 22 P.3d 576 (Colo. App. 2001). This is a higher bar than a simple "prejudice" showing, which benefits the policyholder.

### Colorado Two-Step Late Notice Analysis
*Gregory v. Safeco Ins. Co. of Am.*, 2024 WL 1040531 (Colo. 2024) established the current framework: (1) Was the notice untimely and the delay unreasonable? (2) If so, was the insurer prejudiced? The insured does not lose coverage simply because notice was late — the carrier must clear both hurdles.

### Flood Insurance (Federal — Applies in All States)
- Proof of loss: 60 days from damage. Failure is a bar to recovery. Must be delivered, not mailed.
- Supplemental proofs of loss may be disallowed.
- Suit: Within 1 year of disallowment/denial.
