# Illinois — State PRO Reference
**Source:** Merlin Law Group State Guide (Illinois Edition, Version 2)
**Last verified:** 2026-03-04

## 1. Notice of Loss
**Look to the wording of the policy** — "prompt," "immediate," or "as soon as practicable" all mean notice must be given **within a reasonable time**. Whether notice has been given within a reasonable time is based on the facts and circumstances of the particular case.

**Actual prejudice** to the insurer from late notice is a factor to be considered in determining whether notice was given within a reasonable time.

**Citations:**
- *Barrington Consolidated High School v. American Ins. Co.*, 58 Ill.2d 278 (1974) (reasonable time = facts and circumstances)
- *Country Mutual Ins. Co. v. Livorsi Marine, Inc.*, 222 Ill.2d 303 (2006) (actual prejudice as factor)

## 2. Proof of Loss
**Look to the wording of the policy.** Most policies require submission of a proof of loss within **60 days of the insurer's request**.

**Failure to submit** a proof of loss when requested **can be a bar to recovery**.

**Carrier form-furnishing obligation:** Insurer must provide forms necessary to present claims **within 15 working days** of a request, with such explanations as are necessary to use them effectively. 50 Ill. Admin. Code section 919.80(d)(8)(C); 215 ILCS 5/154.6(o).

**Waiver rules:**
- The proof-of-loss requirement **can be waived** if an insurer denies liability on grounds other than the insured's failure to file a proof of loss.
- Can also be waived if the insurer's conduct leads the insured to believe no formal filing is necessary.

**Citations:**
- *Tarzian v. West Bend Mutual Fire Ins. Co.*, 74 Ill.App.2d 314 (1st Dist. 1966) (failure to submit = bar)
- *Mathis v. Lumbermen's Mut. Cas. Co.*, 354 Ill.App.3d 854 (5th Dist. 2004) (waiver by denial on other grounds)
- *Downing v. Wolverine Ins. Co.*, 62 Ill.App.2d 305 (2d Dist. 1965) (waiver by conduct)

## 4. Carrier Acknowledgment Deadline
The guide addresses this indirectly through the **Improper Claim Practices** statute. It is an improper claim practice to fail to **acknowledge with reasonable promptness** pertinent communications with respect to claims arising under policies.

**Citation:** 215 ILCS 5/154.6(b)

**Note:** The specific number of days is not stated in the guide. The Illinois Administrative Code (50 Ill. Admin. Code Part 919) likely contains specific timeframes — web search required for exact days.

## 5. Carrier Investigation/Payment Deadline
The guide addresses this indirectly through Improper Claim Practices. It is improper to:
- Fail to adopt and implement **reasonable standards for prompt investigation and settlement** of claims. 215 ILCS 5/154.6(c)
- Fail to **affirm or deny coverage** within a reasonable time after proof of loss statements have been completed. 215 ILCS 5/154.6(i)
- Not attempt in good faith to effectuate **prompt, fair and equitable settlement** of claims where liability has become reasonably clear. 215 ILCS 5/154.6(d)

## 6. SOL — Breach of Contract
**Look to the wording of the policy.** Generally, suit must be filed within **1 or 2 years from the date of loss** (contractual limitation period in the policy).

**Tolling rule (critically important):** Whenever a policy contains a provision limiting the period to sue, the running of that period is **tolled from the date proof of loss is filed** until the date the claim is **denied in whole or in part**. 215 ILCS 5/143.1

**Additional tolling:** Absent a formal insurer request for proof of loss, information submitted by the insured apprising the insurer of the nature and extent of the claim **may toll** the suit limitation period.

**Insurer disclosure obligation:** An insurer's failure to comply with the Illinois Administrative Code requirement to **advise the insured** regarding the number of days the limitation period was tolled and how many days remain before expiration can form the basis for the insurer's **waiver** of the time limitation provision. 50 Ill. Admin. Code section 919.80(d)(8)(C).

**Waiver by conduct:** The suit limitation period can be waived if the insurer's conduct leads the insured to reasonably believe that suit need not be filed within the limitation period.

**Citations:**
- 215 ILCS 5/143.1 (tolling provision)
- *Cramer v. Insurance Exchange Agency*, 174 Ill.2d 513 (1996)
- *Dial Corp. v. Marine Office of America*, 318 Ill.App.3d 1056 (1st Dist. 2001)
- *Hines v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 298 Ill.App.3d 585 (4th Dist. 1998) (tolling without formal POL request)
- *Mathis v. Lumbermen's Mutual Casualty Insurance Company*, 354 Ill.App.3d 854 (5th Dist. 2004) (waiver for failure to advise on tolling)
- *Mitchell v. State Farm Fire & Cas. Co.*, 343 Ill.App.3d 281 (4th Dist. 2003) (waiver by conduct)

## 7. SOL — Bad Faith
Illinois does not have a traditional "bad faith" tort cause of action. Instead, it uses **Section 155 of the Illinois Insurance Code** (215 ILCS 5/155) which provides an **extracontractual remedy** for policyholders whose insurer's refusal to recognize liability and pay a claim is "vexatious and unreasonable."

**Statute of limitations for Section 155 claims:** Not specifically stated in the guide — web search required. (Likely tied to the underlying contract claim's limitation period since it is statutory, not tort.)

**Citation:** 215 ILCS 5/155; *Cramer v. Insurance Exchange Agency*, 174 Ill.2d 513 (1996)

## 8. Appraisal
**Enforceable: Yes.** Illinois public policy favors the resolution of disputes through informal procedures like appraisal and arbitration.

**Binding: Not automatically.** Absent policy language that the insured is giving up the right to sue, an appraisal award **does not operate as a final and binding resolution** and does not foreclose an insured from maintaining a legal action.

**Not a condition precedent to suit:** Compliance with the appraisal clause is **not a prerequisite** to suing, to the extent it has not been demanded before suit has been filed. If the demand is only made after the insured has properly sued, a court is not precluded from determining the amount of the loss.

**Standard Fire Policy appraisal procedure:**
- Each party chooses a competent appraiser within **20 days** after receiving a written request.
- If the two appraisers cannot agree on an umpire within **15 days**, a request may be made to have an umpire appointed by a judge of a court of record in Illinois.

**Insurer pays if insured wins:** When an insured requests appraisal under a fire and extended coverage policy (real property used principally for residential purposes up to 4-family dwelling), and the insured's full amount of appraised loss is upheld, the **insured's appraisal fee and umpire appraisal fee are paid by the insurer**. 215 ILCS 5/397.05

**Citations:**
- *Beard v. Mount Carroll Mut. Fire Ins. Co.*, 203 Ill.App.3d 724 (5th Dist. 1990) (favors informal resolution)
- *Hayes v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 722 F.2d 1332 (7th Cir. 1983) (not prerequisite to suit if not demanded pre-suit)
- *DeGroot v. Farmers Mut. Hail Ins. Co. of Iowa*, 267 Ill.App.3d 723 (3d Dist. 1994) (not automatically final/binding)
- 215 ILCS 5/397.05 (insurer pays fees if insured's amount upheld)

## 9. EUO (Examination Under Oath)
**Failure to appear can be a bar to recovery.** The insured must **substantially comply** with the insurer's requests for EUO and/or books and records. If the insured is unable to comply, he/she must provide a **reasonable justification, explanation, or excuse** for the non-compliance.

**Substantial compliance** can be found where the insured makes some effort to comply with the insurer's request for information, but the cooperation is either late or incomplete.

**Special rules:**
- Read the policy — it controls who must appear and whether, in a multiple insured situation, each must appear outside the presence of any other insured.

**Citations:**
- *Gipps Brewing Corp. v. Central Manufacturing Mutual Ins. Co.*, 147 F.2d 6 (7th Cir. 1945) (failure to appear = bar)
- *Horton v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 125 Ill.App.3d 1034 (1st Dist. 1984) (substantial compliance + reasonable justification)
- *Hartshorn v. State Farm Ins. Co.*, 361 Ill.App.3d 731 (2d Dist. 2005) (substantial compliance definition)

## Notes

### Section 155 — Bad Faith / Vexatious and Unreasonable Conduct (215 ILCS 5/155)
This is Illinois's equivalent of a bad faith remedy. A court considers the **totality of the circumstances**, including:
- The insurer's duties under the insurance contract
- The insurer's attitude and motivation for denying or delaying payment
- Whether the insured was forced to sue to recover
- Whether the insured was deprived of the use of his or her property

**Examples of vexatious and unreasonable conduct:**
1. Refusing to pay despite knowledge of liability and knowledge that refusal would cause serious damage
2. Failing to adequately investigate a claim or denying without adequate supporting evidence
3. Failing to evaluate a claim objectively
4. Interpreting policy provisions in an unreasonable manner

**Citations:**
- *McGee v. State Farm Fire and Cas. Co.*, 315 Ill.App.3d 673 (2d Dist. 2000) (totality of circumstances)
- *Emerson v. American Bankers Ins. Co.*, 223 Ill.App.3d 929 (5th Dist. 1992) (examples of conduct)
- *Zagorski v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 2016 IL App (5th) 140056 (improper claim practices as evidence)

### Improper Claim Practices (215 ILCS 5/154.5 and 154.6)
Full list of improper practices that can be used as evidence of vexatious/unreasonable conduct:
- (a) Knowingly misrepresenting relevant facts or policy provisions
- (b) Failing to acknowledge communications with reasonable promptness
- (c) Failing to adopt reasonable standards for prompt investigation and settlement
- (d) Not attempting good faith prompt, fair, and equitable settlement where liability is reasonably clear
- (e) Compelling policyholders to sue by offering substantially less than ultimately recovered
- (f)-(g) Disproportionate complaints/lawsuits
- (h) Refusing to pay without reasonable investigation
- (i) Failing to affirm or deny coverage within reasonable time after proof of loss
- (j) Attempting to settle for less than reasonable, including **unreasonable caps or limits on paint or materials** when estimating
- (k) Settling based on altered application without insured's knowledge
- (l) Making payment without stating which coverage applies
- (m) Duplicative verification/documentation requirements
- (n) Failing to provide reasonable explanation for denial or compromise
- (o) **Failing to provide forms necessary to present claims within 15 working days** of request

### Standard Fire Policy
The Illinois Standard Fire Policy (identical to the 165-line New York form from 1943) requires that fire insurance coverage must be written in a manner which is equivalent to or exceeds the coverage provided by the Standard Fire Policy. *Lundquist v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 314 Ill.App.3d 240 (2d Dist. 2000).

### Unpaid Property Taxes / Demolition Expense
It is unlawful for any insurer to pay a fire/explosion claim exceeding $25,000 for a structure in Illinois until the insurer receives a certificate that there are (a) no delinquent property taxes and (b) no unpaid demolition expenses — or alternatively a certificate with amounts and direction to pay from proceeds. 215 ILCS 5/397.1

### Catastrophic Event Fee Cap
PA may not charge more than **10%** on claims resulting from a catastrophic event, unless approved in writing by the Director. 215 ILCS 5/1570.

### PA Lien Rights
The Insurance Code recognizes that a public adjusting contract creates a **lien enforceable** by a PA against future loss payments. *Golub and Associates, Inc. v. State Farm Fire and Cas. Co.*, 406 Ill.App.3d 1195 (5th Dist. 2011).

### PA Records Retention
Public adjusters must maintain records for **7 years** after termination of the transaction. 215 ILCS 5/1585.

### Flood Insurance (Federal — applies in all states)
- Proof of loss: 60 days from loss. Failure is a bar to recovery.
- Must be submitted on standard FEMA form, completely filled out and notarized.
- Suit must be brought within 1 year after date of written denial.
- *Bullard v. Connor*, 716 F. Supp. 1081 (N.D. Ill. 1989)
