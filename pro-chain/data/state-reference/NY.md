# New York — State PRO Reference
**Source:** Merlin Law Group State Guide (NYC/New York edition)
**Last verified:** 2026-03-04

## 1. Notice of Loss
**Deadline:** Immediate — notice must be given "as soon as is reasonably possible under the circumstances."

- Failure or late notice results in **forfeiture of coverage**.
- The **burden of proving** that delay in giving notice was reasonable falls on the **insured**. *Rushing v. Commercial Cas. Ins. Co.*, 251 N.Y. 302 (N.Y. 1929).

## 2. Proof of Loss
**Deadline:** Per policy provision (commonly 30 days, 60 days, or upon request). Statutory backstop is **60 days** after the insurer requests it and provides the form.

- The insured must furnish proof of loss within **60 days** after the insurer requests it and provides the form. Failure to file within that period gives the insurer an **absolute defense** to the claim. **N.Y. Ins. Law Section 3407.**
- Proof of loss is a **condition precedent** to recovery. *American Ins. Co. v. Fairchild Industries*, 56 F.3d 435 (2nd Cir. 1995). Insurer **need not show prejudice** from non-compliance.
- **Carrier form-furnishing obligation:** Failure to comply with the proof of loss requirement does **not** bar the claim unless the insurer (1) gives **written notice** after the loss requesting a proof of loss AND (2) **supplies a form**. **N.Y. Ins. Law Section 3407(a).** This is a critical protection — if the carrier never sends the form, the insured's failure to submit one is not a valid defense.
- No particular form or content is required (unless the policy specifically provides otherwise) — the proof of loss must only be adequate enough to enable the insurer to consider its rights and liabilities. *Metropolitan Property and Liability Ins. Co. v. Torcivia*, 455 N.Y.S.2d 666 (2d Dep't 1982).

## 4. Carrier Acknowledgment Deadline
**15 business days** from receipt of a claim.

- Within 15 business days, the insurer must **acknowledge receipt** of the claim AND provide the claimant with **notification of all items, statements, and forms required**. **N.Y. Comp. Codes R. & Regs. 11 Sections 216.4(a)–216.5(a).**

## 5. Carrier Investigation / Payment Deadline
**Investigation/Decision:** Within **15 days** after receipt of proof of loss and all requested information, the insurer must advise whether the claim is **accepted or denied**. **N.Y. Comp. Codes R. & Regs. 11 Section 216.6(c).**

- If additional time is needed for investigation, the insurer must provide **written reasons within 15 days** and then **every 90 days thereafter**.
- **Arson exception:** When the insurer has a reasonable basis (supported by specific information) that the claimant caused the loss by arson, the insurer must advise on acceptance or denial within **30 working days**. **N.Y. Ins. Law Section 2601(a)(4).**
- **Undisputed elements:** If no dispute exists as to one or more elements of a claim (even when other elements are disputed), payment for the undisputed element(s) **shall be made** when it can be done without prejudice to the other party. **N.Y. Comp. Codes R. & Regs. 11 Section 216.6(e).**
- **Payment after settlement:** Insurer must pay any amount finally agreed upon in settlement within **5 business days** from receipt of the settlement agreement (or from the date the insured performs any condition in the agreement, whichever is later). **N.Y. Comp. Codes R. & Regs. 11 Section 216.6(f).**
- **No final-settlement checks:** Insurer shall not issue a check that expressly or impliedly states acceptance constitutes a final settlement or release of future obligations. Insurer cannot require a release broader than the scope of the settlement. **N.Y. Comp. Codes R. & Regs. 11 Section 216.6(g).**
- **Disclaimer obligation:** Insurer must notify the insured **in writing** as soon as it determines there was no policy in force or it is disclaiming liability due to a policy breach, and must provide **specific reasons** for the disclaimer. **N.Y. Comp. Codes R. & Regs. 11 Section 216.6(d).**

## 6. SOL — Breach of Contract
**6 years** from date of breach. **N.Y. Civ. Practice Law & Rules Section 213.**

- The limitations period **can be shortened contractually** if the shortened period is not "so short as to be unreasonable." *Carat Diamond Corp. v. Underwriters at Lloyd's*, 506 N.Y.S.2d 708 (1986) — a 12-month period was found to be reasonable.
- **Standard fire policy** requires suit within **24 months** of the loss. **N.Y. Ins. Law Section 3404.**
- A 2-year policy limitation was found **unenforceable** where the policy required replacement before payment but the property could not be replaced within two years. *Executive Plaza, LLC v. Peerless Ins. Co.*, 22 N.Y.3d 511, 982 N.Y.S.2d 826 (2014).
- **Agent negligence:** 3 years from date of injury. **N.Y. Civ. Practice Law & Rules Section 214.**

## 7. SOL — Bad Faith
Not addressed in guide — web search required.

**Note:** New York does not recognize an independent tort of bad faith for first-party insurance claims. Bad faith claims are generally pursued as breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing (contract-based, subject to the 6-year contract SOL) or via N.Y. Ins. Law Section 2601 unfair claim settlement practices (regulatory action, not a private cause of action). The guide references unfair claim settlement practices under N.Y. Ins. Law Section 2601(a)(4) but does not provide a separate bad faith SOL.

## 8. Appraisal
**Enforceable:** Yes. Appraisal clauses are enforceable in New York.

**Binding:** Yes.

**Time limits:** Generally **no fixed statutory time frame** for invoking appraisal. When the policy fails to specify a deadline, the demand must be made **within a reasonable period** depending on the facts of the particular case. *Chainless Cycle Mfg. Co. v. Security Ins. Co.*, 169 N.Y. 304, 310 (1901).

**Reasonableness factors** (three-part test):
1. Whether the appraisal sought is "impractical or impossible" (i.e., would granting it prejudice the insured?).
2. Whether the parties engaged in good-faith negotiations over valuation of the loss prior to the demand.
3. Whether appraisal is desirable or necessary under the circumstances.

*SR Int'l Business Ins. Co. v. World Trade Center Prop., LLC*, 2004 WL 2979790 (S.D.N.Y. Dec. 1, 2004).

**Additional rules:**
- Can be **waived**, but waiver is **not lightly inferred**. The party asserting waiver bears the burden of proof. *Amerex Grp., Inc. v. Lexington Ins. Co.*, 678 F.3d 193, 199 (2d Cir. 2012).
- Appraisal is limited to **factual disputes** over the amount of loss — scope of coverage is a **legal issue** that cannot be determined by appraisal. *Duane Reade Inc. v. St. Paul Fire & Mar. Ins. Co.*, 411 F.3d 384, 389 (2d Cir. 2005).
- Appraisal only applies to disagreements as to **amount of loss or damage** — not where the insurer **denies liability**. *Kawa v. Nationwide Mut. Fire Ins. Co.*, 664 N.Y.S.2d 430, 431 (Sup. Ct. N.Y. County 1997).
- Under the standard fire policy, issues involving ascertaining value of loss and scope/extent of damages are subject to appraisal. **N.Y. Ins. Law Section 3404(e).**
- Appraisal is a **condition precedent** to filing suit if the appraisal clause is invoked. *Buchholz v. U.S. Fire Ins. Co.*, 39 N.Y.S.2d 663 (N.Y.A.D. 1 Dept. 1943).
- An appraisal demand is **not automatically untimely** just because the opposing party has already initiated litigation. *Amerex Grp., Inc. v. Lexington Ins. Co.*, 678 F.3d 193 (2d Cir. 2012).
- Once invoked, look to the **policy** for time requirements on appraiser appointment.

## 9. EUO (Examination Under Oath)
The guide provides limited detail on EUO specifics for New York. Key points:

- **Read the policy carefully** — many policies are now expanding the definition of who the policyholder must provide for EUO.
- **Books & Records:** Insurers have **very broad discretion** in what they can demand the insured produce.
- **Practical tip from Merlin:** Provide everything the insurance company asks for regarding books and records or **risk having the claim denied**.

**Condition precedent to suit?** Not explicitly addressed in guide — web search required. Generally in New York, compliance with EUO provisions is treated as a condition precedent to recovery under the policy.

## Notes

### Unfair Claim Settlement Practices
N.Y. Ins. Law Section 2601(a)(4) defines unfair settlement practices to include "not attempting in good faith to effectuate prompt, fair and equitable settlements of claims submitted in which liability has become reasonably clear." This is a **regulatory provision** (enforced by the Department of Financial Services), not a private cause of action — but it establishes the standard of conduct and can be used strategically in correspondence.

### Rejection Notice Requirements
Any notice rejecting any element of a personal property insurance claim must contain: the insurer's identity, claims processing address, the insured's policy number, the claim number, and a prominently displayed statement directing the insured to the NY Department of Financial Services complaint process. **N.Y. Comp. Codes R. & Regs. 11 Section 216.6(h).** Non-compliance with this requirement can be used to challenge the validity of a denial.

### Public Adjuster Fee Cap
Maximum PA fee in New York is **12.5%** of total recovery. **N.Y. Comp. Codes R. & Regs. 11 Section 25.7.** Any money paid by the insurer to the insured **prior to** execution of the PA contract may not be included in calculating the 12.5% fee. N.Y. General Counsel Opinion No. 7-26-2006.

### Flood Insurance (Federal — NFIP)
The guide includes a flood insurance section (federal rules, not NY-specific):
- Proof of loss must be filed within **60 days** of damage.
- Proof of loss must be **delivered, not mailed**.
- Failure to file is a **bar to recovery**. *Lucien v. U.S. Sec. Ins. Corp.*, 143 Fed. Appx 152 (11th Cir. 2005).
- Suit must be filed within **1 year** of disallowment. *Hairston v. Travelers Cas. & Sur. Co.*, 232 F.3d 1348 (11th Cir. 2000).
- Supplemental proofs of loss submitted after additional damage is discovered **may be disallowed** under the flood policy.

### Key Regulatory Body
New York State Department of Financial Services (DFS). Consumer complaints can be filed at dfs.ny.gov or by writing to the Consumer Assistance Unit at multiple office locations (New York City, Albany, Garden City, Buffalo).
