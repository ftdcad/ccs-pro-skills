# New Jersey — State PRO Reference
**Source:** Merlin Law Group State Guide
**Last verified:** 2026-03-04

## 1. Notice of Loss
**Immediate.** Standard for breach of notice is "reasonableness and good faith." If notice is given as soon as reasonably possible, courts will not find a material breach. Failure to notify the insurer will not bar coverage unless the insurer shows it has been "appreciably prejudiced." *Solvents Recovery Serv. of New England v. Midland Ins. Co.*, 218 N.J. Super. 49 (App. Div. 1987).

## 2. Proof of Loss
**Look to policy provision — 30 days, 60 days, or upon request.** Not a condition precedent unless required by the policy. Under the standard fire policy, failure to file a proof of loss does not prevent recovery unless demanded in writing by the insurer. **N.J.S.A. 17:36-6.** Upon notice of claim, the insurer must promptly provide the claimant with forms and instructions. **N.J. Admin. Code 11:2-17.6(b).**

## 4. Carrier Acknowledgment Deadline
**10 working days** from receipt of a claim, the insurer must acknowledge receipt and provide the address and number of the claims office or representative handling the claim. **N.J. Admin. Code 11:2-17.6(c).**

## 5. Carrier Investigation / Payment Deadline
**30 calendar days** from receipt of a properly executed proof of loss, the insurer must complete its investigation and make payment. **N.J. Admin. Code 11:2-17.7(c)(1).** Additionally, the insurer must give written notice to the claimant at least **60 calendar days** before a statute of limitation or policy time limit may expire. **N.J. Admin. Code 11:2-17.8(e).**

## 6. SOL — Breach of Contract
**6 years** from the date of loss. **N.J.S.A. 2A:14-1.** The limitations period runs from the date of loss. *Peloso v. Hartford Fire Ins. Co.*, 56 N.J. 514 (1970). The limitations period is **tolled** from the date notice of the loss is given until coverage is formally denied. *Gahaney v. State Farm Ins. Co.*, 56 F. Supp. 2d 491 (D.N.J. 1999). The limitations period can be shortened contractually if the period is reasonable. **N.J.S.A. 17:36-5.20.** The standard fire policy requires suit to be commenced within **1 year** of the loss. **N.J.S.A. 17:36-5.20.**

## 8. Appraisal
**Enforceable and binding.** There is usually no time frame for invoking appraisal. Once invoked, look to the policy for the time requirement on appraiser appointment. Appraisal applies even to total losses to determine the value of what was destroyed. *Drescher v. Excelsior Ins. Co.*, 188 F. Supp. 158 (D.N.J. 1960). The **"broad evidence rule"** requires the appraiser to consider every fact and circumstance that would logically tend to a correct estimate of the insured loss, so as to effectuate complete indemnity. *Ward v. Merrimack Mut. Fire Ins. Co.*, 332 N.J. Super. 515 (App. Div. 2000). Appraisal can be waived. The fact that the insurer disputes coverage does not necessarily preclude appraisal. *Ward v. Merrimack*, id. Critical to read the appraisal clause in the policy for notice requirements, time frames, number of appraisers, and requirements for appointment.

## 9. EUO (Examination Under Oath)
**May operate as a condition precedent to coverage.** *Griggs v. Bertram*, 88 N.J. 347 (N.J. 1982). Insurers have very broad discretion in what books and records can be demanded. Many policies are expanding the definition of who the policyholder must provide for EUO. **Practical tip from guide:** Provide everything the insurance company asks for regarding books and records, or run the risk of having the claim denied.

## Notes

### Tolling of Limitations Period
The SOL is tolled from the date notice of loss is given until coverage is formally denied. This is strategically significant — it means the clock stops while the carrier is processing the claim. *Gahaney v. State Farm Ins. Co.*, 56 F. Supp. 2d 491 (D.N.J. 1999).

### Contractual Shortening of SOL
The limitations period can be shortened contractually if reasonable. The standard fire policy shortens it to 1 year from loss. Always check the specific policy language. **N.J.S.A. 17:36-5.20.**

### 60-Day Expiration Warning
Insurers are required to give written notice to the claimant at least 60 calendar days before any statute of limitation or policy time limit may expire. **N.J. Admin. Code 11:2-17.8(e).** Failure to provide this notice is a potential compliance lever.

### Broad Evidence Rule in Appraisal
New Jersey follows the broad evidence rule — appraisers must consider every fact and circumstance logically tending to a correct estimate of the loss to effectuate complete indemnity. This is favorable to policyholders as it prevents carriers from narrowing the scope of appraisal consideration.

### Proof of Loss — Fire Policy Protection
Under the standard fire policy (N.J.S.A. 17:36-6), failure to file a proof of loss does not prevent recovery unless the insurer has demanded it in writing. This is an important procedural shield.

### Flood Insurance — Different Rules
Flood claims under the NFIP have stricter requirements: proof of loss must be filed within 60 days of damage, failure is a bar to recovery, proof of loss must be delivered (not mailed), and supplemental proofs of loss submitted after additional damage is discovered may be disallowed. Suit must be filed within 1 year of disallowment.

### Public Adjuster Solicitation Restriction
PAs may not solicit or enter into a contract with the insured between 6pm and 8am during the 24 hours after a loss has occurred. **N.J. Admin. Code 11:1-37.13(c); N.J.S.A. 17:22B-13(a).**
